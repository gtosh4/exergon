# Against the Storm Market Note

> Date reviewed: 2026-05-20  
> Subject: [Against the Storm](https://store.steampowered.com/app/1336490/Against_the_Storm/) by Eremite Games / Hooded Horse  
> Purpose: summarize player reception, compare against Exergon's design, and extract practical lessons.

## Snapshot

Against the Storm is a roguelite city builder about establishing settlements under escalating pressure from storms, needs, orders, resource scarcity, biome modifiers, and meta-progression. It is the strongest structural reference for Exergon's run-based factory campaign.

At the time of review, Steam listed Against the Storm as released on December 8, 2023, with `Overwhelmingly Positive` English reviews at 95% positive across more than 18,000 reviews and recent reviews `Very Positive` at 88% positive.

## What Players Like

### Run-Based Builder Structure

Players like that Against the Storm captures the most interesting early and midgame parts of a city builder, then ends the settlement before it becomes solved or stagnant. The reset is not a punishment; it is the structure that keeps decision-making fresh.

### Meaningful Constraints

Biomes, species mixes, blueprint offers, orders, cornerstones, hostility, and resource distribution create different problems each settlement. Players respond well because the variance is readable and strategically meaningful.

### Pressure Without Total Loss

The game creates strong pressure through impatience, reputation, storms, hostility, and resolve, but the broader campaign continues after losses. This makes difficulty tolerable across repeated runs.

### Meta-Progression

The Smoldering City upgrades and world-map progression make each run feel connected to a larger arc. Players like that failed or imperfect settlements can still contribute to long-term progress.

### Pacing

Against the Storm is praised for avoiding the late-city sprawl that can make traditional builders drag. It repeatedly puts the player into the interesting part of the genre.

## What Players Dislike

### High Cognitive Load

Some players find the overlapping systems hard to parse: resolve, hostility, species needs, orders, trade, rainpunk, blightrot, glade events, and blueprint choices. The game can be overwhelming before its rhythm clicks.

### Randomness Can Feel Harsh

When blueprint offers, resources, orders, or glade contents do not align, some players feel the run becomes constrained by luck. Fans often see this as adaptation; detractors see it as unfairness.

### Meta-Progression Friction

Some players dislike needing repeated settlement completions to unlock deeper systems or conveniences. Run-based games risk making early repetition feel like a toll.

### UI Density

The game has a lot to communicate. Even with strong tooltips and overlays, players can miss key information or misread why a settlement is failing.

## Comparison To Exergon

| Axis | Against the Storm | Exergon |
|---|---|---|
| Core fantasy | Rebuild civilization through repeated storm-threatened settlements | Decode alien worlds and escape through scientific mastery |
| Structure | Roguelite city-builder settlements | Run-based factory science campaign |
| Run endpoint | Reputation victory before the settlement stagnates | Escape artifact completion |
| Variance | Biomes, races, blueprints, orders, modifiers, glades | Planet physics, tech nodes, unlock vectors, recipe parameters, sites |
| Pressure | Storms, hostility, impatience, resolve | Research scarcity, power transitions, world reactivity, drone risk |
| Meta-progression | City upgrades, map progression, seals | Biomes, run modifiers, codex, scenarios, narrative |
| Main lesson | The reset can be the point | Exergon's runs must feel complete, not truncated |

Against the Storm validates the core premise that a builder can become more replayable by focusing on the high-agency problem-solving phase and ending each run with a concrete victory.

## Lessons For Exergon

### 1. Each Run Needs A Clean Arc

Exergon should not feel like an endless factory that stops. A run should have a beginning, a set of rising constraints, a late-game synthesis problem, and a visible escape event.

### 2. Variance Must Be Legible

Against the Storm works when players can read a biome, species mix, or modifier and form a plan. Exergon must make planet properties and seeded science equally readable.

### 3. Bad Runs Should Still Be Stories

Players tolerate difficult runs when they create memorable adaptation stories. Exergon should make an inefficient or ugly escape feel like a valid completion, not a failed save.

### 4. Do Not Overload The First Run

Against the Storm's density is part of its appeal, but onboarding is demanding. Exergon should introduce only enough science, graph, power, and exploration complexity to produce one early insight.

### 5. Meta-Progression Should Broaden, Not Flatten

Against the Storm's upgrades can make later runs feel richer. Exergon's meta-progression should add new run possibilities while preserving the integrity of each difficulty tier.

## Store And Demo Implications

The demo should prove that a factory run can end satisfyingly. Show the player landing, learning, building, and leaving. The pitch should emphasize that the reset is a launch to the next unknown world, not lost progress.

Potential positioning:

> A factory game structured like the best part of a roguelite city builder: read the world, solve the run, escape, and carry your knowledge forward.

## Open Questions For Exergon

- What is the shortest run that still feels complete?
- What is Exergon's equivalent of a settlement modifier preview?
- How does a failed or ugly run still contribute to long-term knowledge?
- Which systems are hidden from the first run to avoid overload?
